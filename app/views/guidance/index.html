Standards are established norms or requirements. You must meet the relevant standards to ensure the expected quality levels for your service.

By following a common set of standards, we ensure consistency across the department and give ourselves a framework where we can measure compliance.

Standards should be:

  • easily accessible, so that everyone understands what they need to include as part of their delivery needs and can plan for this
  • stated in a clear and simple manner, allowing for objective assessment of compliance
  • dynamic, so may not exist indefinitely as we identify and manage new standards through a lifecycle process

Stages for publishing a standard

content to be worked on

  1. Propose and draft a standard

    A new or revised standard is proposed and drafted using the standard template (Word document - 78.8KB). It details the identified need, scope, and objective of the standard.

    Discussed and ratified to take forward to draft by the Head of Profession or profession-specific working groups.

    This proposal serves as the initial concept for the standard development.

  2. Standards forum review

    The draft, along with feedback from the community, is reviewed by a standards forum.

    This board consists of subject matter experts and key stakeholders who evaluate the standard for accuracy, feasibility, and alignment with department approach to standards.

    A decision is made. The board can approve, reject, or ask for amendments to the standard.

  3. Outcome

    The standards form will provide feedback to the owner and submittor of the standard.

    Approval

    The working group approves the standard.

    At this point, comms and other processes are put in place before final publication.

    Rejection

    If the standard fails to meet criteria during the review, it is rejected.

    The reasons for rejection are provided back to the owner, and the standard may either be abandoned or revised for resubmission.

  4. Published

    Once approved, the standard can be published and made widely available in the Standards manual.

  5. Archived

    Standards no longer required or abandoned, may be unpublished for an indeterminable amount of time and either revised or republished later.

    Standard could be superseded by another standard.
